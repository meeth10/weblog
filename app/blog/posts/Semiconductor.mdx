# Industry X-Ray: Semiconductor Case Study

## Paper Outcomes
1. Translate vague ambitions into clear market fit.  
2. Framework-driven thinking: assess ideas with strategic rigor.  
3. Identify the startup edge and own your niche.  



## Why am I doing this?
As an Electronics and Communication Engineer, I noticed a gap: **engineers don’t know how to evaluate ideas strategically.**  
During my internship at **Ernst & Young** and through learning from Roger Martin and Michael Porter, I realized that the semiconductor industry — often casually referred to as the "chip industry" — is a great lens to apply structured frameworks.  

This paper is my attempt at analyzing it.



## Introduction
The semiconductor industry is one of the most dynamic in the world. Following Moore’s Law, transistor counts on chips are doubling roughly every two years. We’re now talking about **trillion-transistor chips** — numbers larger than the stars in the Milky Way.  

Naturally, this makes the industry lucrative and strategically important. But how do startups fit in? To answer this, we must examine the **value chain**:

- **Design (Fabless, Architecture, RTL, IP)**
- **Fabrication (Silicon wafers, photolithography, doping)**
- **Packaging, Assembly & Testing (ATP)**



## Porter’s Five Forces in Semiconductors

Instead of lumping everything into "the chip industry," I break it down by stage.

### Threat of New Entrants
- **Design**: 3.5 / 5 → Requires deep design talent + costly EDA tools.  
- **Fabrication**: 0 / 5 → Capex $35–43B, high R&D, massive barriers.  
- **ATP**: 3 / 5 → Moderate barriers; automation & certification matter.  

### Bargaining Power of Suppliers
- **Design**: 3 / 5 → Dependence on EDA/IP vendors like Synopsys, Cadence, ARM.  
- **Fabrication**: 4.5 / 5 → Monopoly-like control by suppliers (e.g. ASML).  
- **ATP**: 3 / 5 → Equipment dependency, but moderate.  

### Bargaining Power of Buyers
- **Design**: 4 / 5 → Cost-sensitive business clients (AMD, NVIDIA).  
- **Fabrication**: 3 / 5 → Some buyer power, but not dominant.  
- **ATP**: 4 / 5 → Buyers demand speed, low defects, and cost efficiency.  

### Threat of Substitutes
- **Design**: 3 / 5 → AI not a true substitute for optimized chip design.  
- **Fabrication**: 1 / 5 → No substitute for silicon wafers yet.  
- **ATP**: 2.5 / 5 → Some vertical integration, but limited.  

### Industry Rivalry
- **Design**: 4.5 / 5 → Fierce, global competition.  
- **Fabrication**: 3 / 5 → Few players (TSMC, Intel, Samsung) but intense.  
- **ATP**: 4 / 5 → Heavy competition on cost, quality, speed.  



## First Principles & Margin of Safety

### Design Stage
- Barrier = **talent & proprietary tools**, not capital.  
- Open-source EDA tools could shift dynamics.  
- **Niche markets** offer whitespace opportunities.  
- **Margin of Safety:** Relatively high → low capex, flexible costs, scalability.  

### Fabrication Stage
- Entry virtually impossible for startups (>$30B capex).  
- Supplier monopolies (ASML, photoresists).  
- Buyer power squeezes profits.  
- **Margin of Safety:** None for startups.  

### ATP Stage
- Moderate entry barriers; automation is key.  
- Profits squeezed by buyers unless niche packaging/solutions offered.  
- **Margin of Safety:** Moderate but requires differentiation.  



## Strategic Framework (Roger Martin’s Cascade)

1. **Winning Aspiration** → Dominate a niche (e.g., Edge AI chips).  
2. **Where to Play** → Fabless design, edge AI focus, US market.  
3. **How to Win** → Differentiation, not cost. Bespoke co-design, GenAI-enabled workflows, performance-per-watt edge.  
4. **Capabilities** → Proprietary design templates, domain-specific IP, strong talent.  
5. **Management Systems** → Communication, alignment, execution discipline.  



## Market Outlook
- **Edge AI market CAGR**: ~19% (USA).  
- High demand for AI acceleration, bespoke silicon.  
- Startups should **focus on niches** rather than generic fabless design.  



## Conclusion
This paper is **not** a recommendation to invest in chip companies, but a **framework for engineers**:  

- Use **Porter’s Five Forces** to assess industry segments.  
- Apply **First Principles** to find root causes.  
- Define your **Margin of Safety** before committing.  
- Use **Roger Martin’s strategy cascade** to frame “Where to play & how to win.”  

Startups succeed not just on instinct but on structured, strategic thinking.  
This approach empowers engineers to **think beyond building products — and towards building ventures that last**.  



## Part B: Policy & Action Plan (India’s Case)
India has tried semiconductor plays before (SCL). To succeed this time, an **ecosystem approach** is needed:

### Phase 0–3 Years: Growth
- Focus on **Design + ATP** (fabless, OSAT support).  
- Subsidies: 30% setup funding, free electricity, incentives.  
- Geographic hubs: Hyderabad (design), MP/Maharashtra (ATP).  

### Phase 3–5 Years: Middle
- Quarterly surveys, policy tweaks.  
- Collaborations with IITs for talent & EDA access.  
- Build a **joint fab facility** with global players.  

### End Phase
- Transition to local fabs with ecosystem support (chemicals, water, electricity).  
- Continuous protection for design innovation (AI, edge computing niches).
