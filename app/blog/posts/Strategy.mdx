I’ve always been intrigued by how companies safeguard themselves, grow, and sometimes *completely outshine* their rivals. Why does one startup break through while another fades into the noise? Why do some corporations become too rigid, while others reinvent themselves?

That curiosity led me to a fancy word: **strategy**.  

At first, it sounded like corporate jargon. But the more I read, the more I realized it’s the invisible backbone of every decision that matters. I dove in like a madman — searching, questioning, peeling back the layers — until I stumbled across Roger Martin’s work.  

And oh my god, his take on strategy clicked with me instantly. As an engineer, I’ve always believed in building things, in execution, in making it real. Martin argues the same: strategy isn’t about buzzwords or slide decks. It’s about **choices** — *what to do, where to play, how to win, and most importantly, why*.  

That framework separates the companies that drift with the market from the ones that actually shape it. It’s what makes you distinct in the endless rat race of startups and the suffocating rigidity of corporations.  

Of course, it’s more complicated than it looks at first glance. Strategy is less about grand plans and more about constant clarity: making the hard calls, saying no to good options to protect the great ones, and sticking to a direction even when the noise around you is deafening.  

For me, discovering Martin’s view on strategy wasn’t just academic. It aligned with how I see engineering and execution — strategy as the blueprint, execution as the build. Without one, the other collapses. Together, they make companies not just survive, but stand out.
